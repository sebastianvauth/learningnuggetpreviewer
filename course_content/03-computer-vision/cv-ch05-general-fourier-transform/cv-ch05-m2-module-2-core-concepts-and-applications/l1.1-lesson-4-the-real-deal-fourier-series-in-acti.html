The Fourier Series Formulas

For any periodic signal y(t) that repeats every period T, we can represent it with this sum:

y(t) = a_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \left( a_k \cos(k\omega_0 t) + b_k \sin(k\omega_0 t) \right)

This formula says our signal y(t) is equal to an average value (a_0) plus a sum of cosine waves and sine waves at harmonic frequencies (k\omega_0). Our job is to find the values of the coefficients a_0, a_k, and b_k. These numbers are the recipe!

Example 1: A Simple Sine Wave

Let's start with an easy one to build our confidence. What's the recipe for a simple sine wave, y(t) = \sin(\omega_0 t)? Intuitively, the recipe should just be '1 part sine wave at frequency \omega_0, and nothing else.' Let's see if the math agrees.

Calculate a_0 (Average Value): a_0 = \frac{1}{T} \int_{0}^{T} \sin(\omega_0 t) \, dt = 0

The average value of a sine wave over a full period is zero. This makes sense; there's no DC offset.

Calculate a_k (Cosine Parts): a_k = \frac{2}{T} \int_{0}^{T} \sin(\omega_0 t) \cos(k\omega_0 t) \, dt = 0

Due to a property called orthogonality, the integral of a sine wave multiplied by a cosine wave over a period is always zero. No cosine parts needed!

Calculate b_k (Sine Parts): b_k = \frac{2}{T} \int_{0}^{T} \sin(\omega_0 t) \sin(k\omega_0 t) \, dt = \begin{cases} 1 & \\text{if } k=1 \\\\ 0 & \\text{if } k \\neq 1 \\end{cases}

Again, due to orthogonality, this integral is zero unless the frequencies are the same (k=1). When k=1, the integral evaluates to T/2, and the whole expression becomes 1.

The only non-zero coefficient is b_1 = 1. The math confirms it! The recipe is exactly what we thought.

Example 2: The Square Wave

Now for the main event. Let's analyze the symmetric square wave that goes from +1 (from t=0 to t=1) to -1 (from t=1 to t=2). Its period is T=2, and its fundamental frequency is \omega_0 = \pi.

Image placeholder: Description: A clear plot of one period of the symmetric square wave being analyzed.

1. Calculate a_0: a_0 = \frac{1}{2} \left( \int_{0}^{1} (1) \, dt + \int_{1}^{2} (-1) \, dt \right) = \frac{1}{2} (1 - 1) = 0

The area of the positive part cancels the area of the negative part. The average value is zero.

2. Calculate a_k: a_k = \frac{2}{2} \int_{0}^{2} y(t) \cos(k\pi t) \, dt = 0

This requires a bit more work, but our square wave is an 'odd' function and cosine is an 'even' function. The product of an odd and even function is odd, and integrating an odd function over a symmetric interval always gives zero. No cosine components!

3. Calculate b_k: b_k = \frac{2}{2} \left( \int_{0}^{1} (1)\\sin(k\\pi t)\\,dt + \\int_{1}^{2} (-1)\\sin(k\\pi t)\\,dt \\right)

After performing the integration and plugging in the limits (a bit of calculus work!), we find a pattern...

4. The Result for b_k: b_k = \\begin{cases} \\frac{4}{k\\pi} & \\text{if } k \\text{ is odd} \\\\ 0 & \\text{if } k \\text{ is even} \\end{cases}

The sine components are only non-zero for the odd harmonics (k=1, 3, 5...)! And their amplitude decreases as k gets bigger.

So the final recipe for our square wave is:

$$ y(t) = \\frac{4}{\\pi}\\sin(\\pi t) + \\frac{4}{3\\pi}\\sin(3\\pi t) + \\frac{4}{5\\pi}\\sin(5\\pi t) + \\dots $$

This is the exact mathematical recipe we saw visually in Lesson 1! The math has proven our intuition correct.

Why It Matters

Being able to perform this calculation is the proof in the pudding. It demonstrates that the beautiful idea of signal decomposition isn't just a concept; it's a rigorous, predictive mathematical tool. These coefficients we calculated are exactly the values a computer, running an algorithm like the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), would find to analyze or reconstruct a signal. This is the solid ground upon which techniques like JPEG and audio processing are built.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between the Fourier Series and the general Fourier Transform again?

Great question to clarify! The Fourier Series (which we used today) is specifically for periodic signals—signals that repeat in a predictable cycle. It gives you a discrete set of coefficients for frequencies that are integer multiples of the fundamental. The Fourier Transform is the more general tool that works for any signal, even non-repeating ones (it treats them as if their period is infinitely long). It gives you a continuous frequency spectrum. For digital work like images, we use a discrete version of the general transform.
