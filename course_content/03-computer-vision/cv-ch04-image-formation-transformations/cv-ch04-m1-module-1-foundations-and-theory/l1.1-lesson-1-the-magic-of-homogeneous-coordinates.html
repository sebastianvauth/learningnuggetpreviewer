A New Dimension of Possibilities

Imagine you have a point on a piece of paper, say at coordinates (2, 5). This is its home in the 2D world. We call these familiar coordinates inhomogeneous or Cartesian coordinates.

Continue

Now, for our magic trick, we're going to give this point a little boost. We'll lift it into a 3D world by simply adding a third number, a '1', at the end. So, our point (2, 5) now becomes (2, 5, 1).

Continue

This new representation, (x, y, 1), is called an augmented vector, and it's our first step into the world of homogeneous coordinates. It seems simple, but this extra number is about to give us superpowers.

Here’s where it gets really interesting. In this new 3D space, any point that lies on the straight line connecting our new point (2, 5, 1) to the origin (0, 0, 0) represents the exact same 2D point (2, 5).

Continue

Understanding the Math

Let's break this down. A 2D point \((x, y)\) is represented in homogeneous coordinates by a 3-element vector \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\):

\[\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = (x', y', w)^T = (wx,\; wy,\; w)^T\]

Here, w is any non-zero number. It's a scaling factor. Think of it as a 'zoom' level in this 3D space.

Continue

For example, let's take our point (2, 5).
If we choose w=1, we get (2, 5, 1).
If we choose w=2, we get (2*2, 5*2, 2) = (4, 10, 2).
If we choose w=0.5, we get (2*0.5, 5*0.5, 0.5) = (1, 2.5, 0.5).
All three of these homogeneous vectors—(2, 5, 1), (4, 10, 2), and (1, 2.5, 0.5)—represent the exact same 2D point!

Getting Back to Reality: Back Projection

So, how do we get back from this magical 3D space to our familiar 2D paper? We perform back projection. It’s simple: just divide the first two numbers by the third one (w).

\[x = \frac{x'}{w},\quad y = \frac{y'}{w}\]

Let's test it with our example (4, 10, 2):
x = 4/2 = 2
y = 10/2 = 5
And we're back to our original point, (2, 5)! It works!

Build Your Vocab

Homogeneous Coordinates

A system that represents points in an N-dimensional space using N+1 coordinates. For 2D, we use 3 coordinates.

Inhomogeneous Coordinates

Your standard Cartesian coordinates, like (x, y).

Augmented Vector

The most common homogeneous representation, where we simply add a '1' at the end: (x, y) becomes (x, y, 1).

Back Projection

The process of converting homogeneous coordinates back to Cartesian by dividing by the last coordinate (w).

Why does this matter?

This might seem like just a math trick, but it's the key that unlocks our ability to represent all the complex geometric transformations we'll see—from simple shifts to perspective distortion—with a single, elegant tool: matrix multiplication. It turns messy, separate problems into one unified, beautiful solution.

Stop and Think

In the back-projection formula, we divide by \(w\). We said \(w\) must be non-zero. But what if it was zero? What would that mean geometrically?

Think about parallel lines. Where do they meet? At a place we call 'infinity'. Points with w=0 are special; they are called 'points at infinity' and they represent directions rather than locations. For example, all horizontal lines in a 2D plane meet at the same point at infinity.

Frequently Asked

Why go through all this trouble to add an extra dimension? Isn't that more complicated?

It seems more complicated at first, but it makes the operations we want to perform much simpler. Instead of having separate rules for shifting, rotating, and scaling, we can now do everything with one matrix. For a computer, especially a GPU designed for matrix math, this is a massive simplification and speed-up!
